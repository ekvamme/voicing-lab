# Public-Domain Jazz Standards — research for Voicing Lab

*Compiled 2026-07-02. Purpose: identify popular standards whose **compositions** are out of
copyright in the US, so the app can legally show actual harmony (and eventually melody), not
just reduced chord changes. This is research, not legal advice.*

---

## The rules (US, as of 2026)

- A musical **composition** published in **1930 or earlier** is in the US public domain.
  Works published before 1978 get 95 years from publication; the 1930 class entered PD on
  Jan 1, 2026. Each new year unlocks another year (1931 → Jan 1, 2027, etc.).
- **Publication date, not composition/recording date, is what counts.** The classic trap:
  *Mood Indigo* was recorded in Oct 1930, but its sheet music was published in **1931**, so it
  is *not* PD until 2027. Verify sheet-music publication year per tune before shipping it
  (Catalog of Copyright Entries; jazzstandards.com and Wikipedia usually note it).
- **Composition ≠ recording.** Only sound recordings from **1925 or earlier** are PD. Use the
  app's own audio (it already synthesizes everything) — never sample records.
- **Composition ≠ arrangement.** The old bootleg Real Books (the REALBK PDFs in this folder)
  contain edited/reharmonized charts; a distinctive published arrangement can carry its own
  copyright even when the song is PD. Safest source for "actual harmony" is the **original
  published sheet music**, which for PD tunes is freely scannable/downloadable (see Sources
  for sheet music below).
- **Chord progressions alone are generally not protectable** — that's why fake-book changes
  circulate. The protected core is the **melody** (and lyrics). Practical consequence for the
  app: chord-symbol reductions of newer tunes (what it does today) are low-risk; displaying
  **melody** is what requires the tune to be PD.
- **US-only.** Most other countries use life-of-author + 50/70 years. Fine print if the app
  ever ships internationally: George Gershwin (d. 1937) cleared life+70 in 2008, but Hoagy
  Carmichael (d. 1981) is protected in life+70 countries until 2052. For a personal/US
  release this doesn't bite.

## Already PD (published 1929 and earlier) — the pedagogical picks

Years are standard reference years; verify publication before shipping any single tune.

**1929** — Ain't Misbehavin' (Waller); Honeysuckle Rose (Waller) — *the* ii–V study;
What Is This Thing Called Love (Porter) — minor ii–V workout; Star Dust (Carmichael; music
1927/29, Parish lyric 1929); More Than You Know (Youmans); Without a Song (Youmans);
Am I Blue; (What Did I Do to Be So) Black and Blue (Waller); Mean to Me; Just You, Just Me;
Singin' in the Rain.

**1928** — I Can't Give You Anything but Love (McHugh/Fields); Sweet Lorraine; Basin Street
Blues (Spencer Williams); Crazy Rhythm; Makin' Whoopee; She's Funny That Way; If I Had You;
Nagasaki.

**1927** — Blue Skies (Berlin) — gorgeous minor→major study; My Blue Heaven; 'S Wonderful
(Gershwin); Ain't She Sweet; The Best Things in Life Are Free; Black and Tan Fantasy
(Ellington/Miley).

**1926** — Bye Bye Blackbird — beginner classic; Someone to Watch Over Me (Gershwin);
Baby Face; Muskrat Ramble; When the Red, Red Robin.

**1925** — **Sweet Georgia Brown** (Pinkard) — pure cycle-of-dominants, tailor-made for the
circle-of-fifths panel; Dinah; Five Foot Two, Eyes of Blue; Always (Berlin); Squeeze Me
(Waller).

**1924** — Tea for Two (Youmans) — famous ii–V oscillation; Oh, Lady Be Good!; Fascinating
Rhythm; The Man I Love (Gershwin) — chromatic descent study; It Had to Be You (Jones);
I'll See You in My Dreams; Somebody Loves Me; Everybody Loves My Baby; California Here I

contrafact source for "Donna Lee"; Tiger Rag (1917); After You've Gone (1918); Ja-Da (1918);
Royal Garden Blues (1919); Avalon (1920) — cycle tune; Whispering (1920) — contrafact source
for "Groovin' High"; Margie (1920); The Sheik of Araby (1921); Limehouse Blues (1922);
Chicago (1922); China Boy (1922); Way Down Yonder in New Orleans (1922); Charleston (1923);
Tin Roof Blues (1923); Who's Sorry Now (1923).

## Sources for actual PD harmony (original sheet music scans)

- **Lester S. Levy Sheet Music Collection** (Johns Hopkins) — huge, downloadable, well indexed.
- **Library of Congress Notated Music** / **IMSLP** — both carry 1910s–20s popular sheet music.
- **Internet Archive** — scanned song folios; also PD-era recordings for reference listening.
- **UMaine Vocal Popular Sheet Music Collection** — includes copyright-page scans (handy for
  verifying publication years).
- jazzstandards.com — publication years, histories, and form analyses per tune.

## What "actual harmony" means for the engine

The app currently reduces everything to four qualities (maj7, m7, dom7, m7♭5). Real
1920s–30s sheet-music harmony needs, roughly in order of frequency:

1. **6th chords** (C6, Cm6) — the era's default tonic color (4 notes; engine-compatible as-is).
2. **dim7** (°7) — passing chords everywhere in Georgia, Honeysuckle Rose (4 notes; symmetric,
   so `invIndex` still works but all inversions are the same pc-set — label carefully).
3. **Augmented / 7♯5** — V+ cadences (4 notes as 7♯5).
4. **Dominant alterations & extensions** (7♭9, 9, 13) — 5+ notes; either keep a 4-voice
   reduction toggle ("as written" vs "4-voice") or extend `voiceLead` beyond `P4` perms
   (cost grows factorially: 5 voices = 120 perms, still trivial).
5. **sus4 / 6-5 and 4-3 suspensions** — idiomatic cadences.

Items 1–3 are 4-note chords: only the `QUAL`/`QLAB` tables and the spelling logic need
extending. That covers a large majority of what the 1920s tunes actually need. A separate
"as written" mode could show the published piano voicings verbatim later; melody display
ties into the existing "melody-on-top lock" roadmap item.

## Suggested first batch for the app

Ordered for teaching value and engine fit (mostly 4-note-friendly):

1. **Sweet Georgia Brown** (1925) — dominants around the circle; the cof panel shines.
2. **I Got Rhythm** (1930) — rhythm changes; add a 6th-chord quality first.
3. **Honeysuckle Rose** (1929) — ii–V drill.
4. **Tea for Two** (1924) — ii–V oscillation, beginner-friendly.
5. **After You've Gone** (1918) — early-jazz harmony, iv–I motion.
6. **Body and Soul** (1930) — the bridge is a masterclass for the key-shift + travel meter.
7. **Georgia on My Mind** (1930) — needs dim7; the richest payoff for "actual harmony."
